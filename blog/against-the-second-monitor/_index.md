# Against the Second Monitor
<time datetime="2026-07-09">Jul, 9 2026</time>

Today I decided to turn off my second monitor and it was great for focus.
Normally, I'll have my browser and terminal (emulator) open with each taking up
an entire display (sometimes splitting my second display vertically for music).

By only using one monitor, my eyes never drifted over if I got bored or to some
sort of stutter/stop in my work. This prevents the "getting distracted" problem
of having two monitors that you might've heard before.

However, my favorite advantage* of one monitor is the **increased effort needed
to view another window**. Having whatever I am working on fullscreen means I'd
have to `alt+tab` to view another window. The greater physical effort locks my
brain in sort of "purist mode" where I tend to seek information in ways that are
already at my fingertips with my Neovim[^neovim] workflow.

[^neovim]: [Neovim](https://neovim.io/)

For example, today I spent my time learning how nixd[^nixd] works by tracing the
source code and callstack(s) of what I was looking at. This allowed me to
efficiently read documentation, jump around the project, and edit files all
without waiting for web pages to load.

[^nixd]: [github:nix-community/nixd - Nix language server, based on nix libraries](https://github.com/nix-community/nixd)

This can be argued as "yeah that's why nvim is goated bro" regardless of a second
monitor's state. While I do agree with this, I want to emphasize that **there's a
certain beauty of doing everything within the terminal** (extra points if only
one tmux[^tmux] pane) that goes away if you have a second window visible.

[^tmux]: [tmux - The Terminal Multiplexer for Developers](https://tmux.app/)

This workflow inherently reduces the "drug" that is AI being able to quickly
present whatever information you're seeking in whatever format you want. We all
know the effects on our learning process that AI has no reason to go deeper than
that...

Throughout most of my time at school, this was how I worked every day. In fact,
I was almost never in my apartment/dorm while working/programming and spent most
of my time in a lab, study room, or at a friend's place.

This forced me to use my laptop, which has a 14" 1600x900 230 Nit LED backlight
display[^T420s]. Even if I had the luxury of another monitor to connect to, I
would never use my laptop as a second display. 

[^T420s]: [T420s - ThinkPad-Wiki](https://thinkwiki.de/T420s)

> The external display I *did* connect to was 16:10 and was PERFECT for
> splitting two 80 char width windows with line numbers and
> sign-columns[^sign-columns] at a comfortable font size (I still want one to
> this day).

[^sign-columns]: [sign-column - Neovim docs](https://neovim.io/doc/user/sign/#sign-column)

Once I graduated, I returned home to a proper setup and used both my monitors
since I forgot how useful it could be. I won't go into the benefits there, that's
not what this blog is about but I find myself drifting back and forth between
one monitor and two monitor usage. I rediscovered why I love using only one
monitor and that led me to write this blog post.
